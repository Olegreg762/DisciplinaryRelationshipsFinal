A historically significant consequence of the 9/11 attacks was the transformation of terrorism from a solely criminal issue to one that involved military action. Previous terror attacks had been handled by law enforcement, courts and intelligence agencies. However, after the attacks of 9/11 the United States declared a Global War on Terror; this justified the military action and the increased security measures at home. The war was also used as justification for the invocation of NATO Article 5, which was and remains the first time this article has been invoked. The USA PATRIOT Act, a landmark security legislation that came as a response to the attacks. The act increased the surveillance capabilities for law enforcement and allowed these agencies greater access to personal communications and financial records. These were viewed by many as necessary for public safety but critics argued they threatened civil liberties and privacy rights. Detention centers such as Guantanamo Bay and the use of what was termed as “enhanced interrogation techniques”, which was the use of torture in interrogation are other controversial consequences of the attacks. These policies sparked an ongoing debate on how the United States handles human rights, national security, and limits to governmental power. 9/11 marked a turning point in how the United States balanced security and freedom and is a debate that continues today.
