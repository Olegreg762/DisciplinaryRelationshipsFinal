The impact 9/11 had on the arts changed over time significantly. The earliest response focused on the shock and destruction caused by the attacks. Artists attempted to capture the emotional impact of witnessing the collapse of the Twin Towers and the sense of uncertainty that followed. Artistic works began to shift away from direct depictions of the attacks as the War on Terror progressed. They began to explore broader themes related to terrorism, conflict and political violence. Some works portrayed terrorists via satire and caricatures to reflect the cultural anxieties regarding terrorism. Jones and Smith noted that as violence against artists portraying Islam and Muslim prophets through these means increased, the responses became increasingly ambiguous. They suggest that artists avoided making clear moral judgments about terrorism and Islam and instead chose to produce works that reflected uncertainty and complexity. This trend was labeled by the authors as the “age of ambiguity,” where artistic expression often mirrored broader social and political uncertainty about the War on Terror. These evolving responses reflected how society’s understanding of the 9/11 attacks changed over two decades. Rather than simply preserving memory, artistic works became a part of the ongoing cultural debate about terrorism, war, and political responsibility.
